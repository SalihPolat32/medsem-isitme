Blog

Key points to know when choosing and using hearing aids.

Signs of hearing loss

Turning up the TV, frequent “can you repeat that?”, difficulty in noise...

Read more
  • Difficulty distinguishing speech in crowded places.
  • Missing words during phone calls.
  • Needing higher TV/phone volume than others.
  • Fatigue: increased listening effort by the end of the day.

If you notice these signs, the best next step is to confirm with measurements and determine the right solution.

Behind-the-ear vs in-the-ear: pros / cons

Selection is based on aesthetics, power needs, ease of use and ear anatomy.

Read more
Behind-the-ear (BTE/RIC) — generally more powerful and flexible
  • Pro: Wider power range; adaptable to different levels of loss.
  • Pro: Controls can be easier to operate; practical for daily use.
  • Pro: Better ventilation; easier receiver/dome/tube replacement.
  • Con: Body is visible behind the ear; may contact glasses/masks.
In-the-ear (ITE/ITC/CIC/IIC) — more compact and “discreet” options
  • Pro: Less visible; preferred when aesthetics are a priority.
  • Pro: Microphones sit closer to the natural ear position, which can support more natural directionality in some cases.
  • Con: More exposed to earwax/moisture; filter/cleaning needs may increase.
  • Con: Power options can be limited for very advanced losses (model-dependent).
Note: The best choice is determined with audiological testing plus ear canal anatomy and your daily listening needs.

First adaptation period

Your brain “relearns”. Gradual use and regular follow-ups deliver the best results.

Read more
  • Week 1: Start with home listening and short outdoor trials.
  • Weeks 2–4: Increase time in noisy places; personalize with fine-tuning.
  • Follow-up: A few short check-ups in the first month can significantly improve comfort.

Early use: SD test & perception support

Early, properly fitted use can offer advantages for speech understanding and brain adaptation.

Read more
SD (Speech Discrimination) / Speech Understanding
  • SD test: Measures how accurately you recognize speech/words (often as a percentage).
  • Early intervention: If auditory input remains low for a long time, listening effort may increase; early support can ease adaptation.
  • Goal: Improve access to speech cues and accelerate relearning.
Perception in daily life
  • First days: Some sounds (paper rustling, dishes) may feel prominent; this is often normal.
  • Gradual use: Increasing usage time step-by-step and regular fine-tuning generally improves outcomes.
  • Follow-up: Fitting, dome/mold selection and program adjustments can affect both SD and comfort.
Note: SD scores vary with type/severity of loss, duration of hearing difficulty and test conditions. The target is the best balance of clarity and comfort.

Care: filters & cleaning

Regular care helps maintain performance and reduces failure risk.

Read more
  • Moisture/dust: Store in a clean, dry place; consider a drying kit when needed.
  • Filters: Earwax buildup can reduce sound; periodic replacement is recommended.
  • Check: If volume drops, first inspect dome/filter/cleanliness.

Bluetooth & connectivity

Your phone model, OS and device family determine the experience.

Read more
  • Audio streaming: Calls, music and video audio to the hearing aids (compatibility-dependent).
  • Hands-free: In some combinations, talk without touching the phone (model-dependent).
  • LE Audio / Auracast: Next-gen broadcast/streaming on supported models (model-dependent).

Frequently Asked Questions

Audiological measurements (audiogram), degree of hearing loss and lifestyle needs are evaluated together. Ear anatomy, ease of use and connectivity expectations also influence the decision.

The brain “relearns” new sounds. Environmental sounds can feel different at first. With gradual use and regular follow-ups, most users adapt noticeably within a few weeks.

The goal is not only volume. Proper fitting prioritizes speech and manages noise to improve understanding, which is why accurate programming is critical.

There is no single “best”. Behind-the-ear models often provide a wider power range and easier handling. In-the-ear models can offer aesthetic advantages. The choice is based on loss severity, ear canal shape and usage preferences.

Rechargeable models provide convenience and can offer all-day use with quick charging. Battery-powered models offer simple replacement and can be flexible for some users. We help you decide based on your routine.

Many models include Bluetooth, but support depends on phone model/OS and the device family’s connectivity standard. We check compatibility and set up the best match.

Feedback is usually caused by dome/mold fit, earwax, loose placement or incorrect settings. With proper coupling and feedback control, it can often be resolved quickly.

Daily surface cleaning and moisture protection improve performance. For in-the-ear devices, filters/guards may need periodic replacement depending on usage and earwax. We guide you during follow-ups.

In the first period, more frequent follow-ups are recommended to speed adaptation. Afterwards, a periodic check every 6–12 months is sufficient for most users, and earlier visits are possible if needed.

Warranty terms can vary by model and supply channel. We perform diagnostics and guide you through service; repair or part replacement is planned based on device status.
